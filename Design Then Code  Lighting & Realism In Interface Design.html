Lighting & Realism In Interface Design by Mike Rundle

Designing great user interfaces is an art form but there are real, concrete methods by which pixels are pushed and realistic interfaces are created. Poor designers slap together gradients and drop shadows, but true interface craftsmen understand why techniques are used and how to create textured, realistic widgets that appear to jump off the screen. This guide aims to explain why some interfaces look great while others look inconsistent, how to craft beautiful interface widgets in Photoshop, and the theory behind the design techniques.

The Physics Of A Light Source

Objects in the real world are three dimensional, but you can only really tell that they have three dimensions when a light source is hitting them, exposing their volume and size to your eye. The ever-present light source on our planet is the Sun, and as it casts rays to the Earth's surface, these rays come in contact with objects and interact with them in some predictable ways.

In this picture, the Sun would be high in the sky, a bit to the left of the golf ball. You can tell where the Sun is by looking at the highlights on the golf ball and where the shadow is on the ground. Here the brightest highlights are on the top left of the ball, and the cast shadow is to the right. The underside of the ball closest to the shadow is darker because the ball has blocked much of the sunlight.

The reason that the side of the ball closest to the Sun is brighter is because the Sun's rays are reflecting off it and going directly into the sensor of the digital camera that took this photograph. The underside of the ball is darker because fewer rays are reaching it, thus, fewer rays are reflecting off that side of the ball into the camera. The shadow that the ball is casting onto the grass is not only darker, but the green's vibrance has been significantly reduced as well. Normally, grass appears green because it is absorbing all colors of the visible spectrum except green which it reflects. In this case, far fewer light rays reach that area of the grass so much less green is reflected.

But, wait! The surface of the golf ball isn't totally smooth, it's filled with dimples. Let's take a closer look at one of those dimples and examine exactly how light is playing with its surface.

This is a closer look at the dimples on the ball. First, take a look at the line I drew that indicates where the Sun is probably located. This position (ahem, not to scale) was determined by looking at the brightest, most highlighted part of the ball and then drawing an imaginary line back to the darkest area of the shadow at the base of the ball. The angle of this line, if extended all the way into the sky, would point somewhere near the area where the actual Sun is located.

Now let's talk about the dimple that I've indicated with two arrows. In case you've never seen or felt a golf ball before, dimples on golf balls are like little craters: indented, circular regions on the surface of the ball. What if you had no idea what a golf ball was or what dimples were, let alone if they were indented or poked out from the surface? Would you be able to figure this out from this image? The dimple in question has a highlight on the bottom edge and a shadow on the top edge, which is opposite from the overall golf ball which has its highlight on the top and shadow on the bottom. The shadow on the top edge of the dimple exists because that little sliver sits below the surface compared to the raised edge of the dimple. The raised edge, at that exact region, is blocking the Sun's rays like an awning off a building that blocks the rain. Because light can't hit that top area of the dimple like it does the rest of the ball, it causes a shadow at the top inner lip. The bottom lip is exactly the opposite: it's rising up and poking out to get back to the normal surface of the ball, and this raised lip is reflecting more of the Sun's rays so it's much brighter than the surrounding surface outside the dimple.

Shadows are caused by a light source being blocked by things like overhangs and other objects. Highlights are caused because a surface pokes out closer towards the light source and can reflect more of its light — like snow only falling on a mountain peak because it's closer to the colder regions of the atmosphere. In the real, physical world, everything has shadows and highlights and their location is determined by the location of the light source. If this photo were taken at a different time of day, the Sun would be in a different location in the sky and a different region of the ball would be highlighted. Sure enough, the shadow would move too, and all the dimple highlights and locations would shift.

Lighting Used In Interface Design

The underlying secret to beautiful user interface design is realism: making 2D objects on your screen appear to sit in 3D space with volume, highlights and shadows that might appear in real life. The easiest way to convey to a user that something has weight and physicality is to pretend there's a light source in your computer screen that's shining down on everything. Here's Apple's take on this concept from their Human Interface Guidelines.

All interface elements have a common light source from directly above.

Straight from the horse's mouth. Apple suggests that you design your user interface in a manner that depicts a light source directly at the top of your computer screen that shines 90° straight down onto all elements. What does this mean?

  • Elements that are intended to appear "popped out" towards the user's eye will have a highlight on the top and a shadow on the bottom.
  • Elements that are intended to appear sunken down, deeper into the screen, will have a shadow at the top and a highlight on the bottom.

From the golf ball example earlier, the parts of the surface that are closer to the Sun were brighter than the surfaces further away or shielded from the Sun's rays. The parts that were shielded (due to an object in the way, like the golf ball itself, or a little ledge poking out, like the top lip of the dimple) are shadowed because they receive and reflect fewer rays of light than other areas of the surface.

Interface elements that bulge out from the screen will be lit like the golf ball: highlight on the top section and a shadow on the bottom. Interface elements that are sunken into the screen are more like the dimples: the ledge at the top causes a shadow at the top inner lip, and then as the bottom lip rises out towards the user's eye, it catches the faux light source and is highlighted.

Twitter For Mac

Now let's take a look at a real interface component that was designed with the Apple-suggested 90° light source in mind. This is the new tweet window from Twitter for Mac.

Here's a general overview of the design of this window:

  • The content area has a slight gray-to-white highlight on it (lighter part at the top) indicating the surface was meant to look ever-so-slightly bulged out towards the user.
  • The profile image has a dark shadow at the top and a slight highlight at the bottom, indicating that the image is sunken into the screen.
  • The bottom actions area is dark gray, but this also has a slight gradient applied and has a lighter area on the top.
  • Both the Cancel and Tweet buttons have a gradient and appear to be bulged out off the screen.

As you can see, this window may look simple but is actually composed of a number of different surfaces, each designed to respect the main, global light source. Most surfaces are meant to look bulged out, but the profile image is designed to look sunken in which gives a nice balance to the overall "depth" of this design.

Let's zoom in on the Tweet button and bottom section to dissect the design further.

First, note the transition between the lighter content area and the bottom actions area. There's a stark black line, then a light gray line, then it goes to a medium gray. If this were a real, physical surface and you looked at it from the side, it'd look like a <, a small v-shaped indentation in the surface indicating one panel stopping and the next beginning. Like a gap in a laptop casing. Because this design exists within the context of a top light source, the single pixel black line is the top lip shadow (like the dimple on the golf ball) and the bottom, lighter line is the lip highlight as the edge transitions up off the screen, closer to the user's eye. Then, right after the lip highlight, the normal surface of the actions area begins and is uses a very slight gradient from dark gray to darker gray, so it's designed to bulge out just a small amount.

Although these are just single-pixel lines next to each other (that look a little odd if you're looking at them while zoomed in) when you view the design from further back it really looks like a little indented gap. If the two lines were flipped then it just wouldn't look right, even though it's only a few pixels that are incorrect.

Artificial Light Sources

Although the main, global light source is shining directly down on all objects in your interface, that doesn't mean you can't artificially create additional subtle light sources within your design. The tweet button is a good example: its slight outer blue glow indicates that it's emitting a small amount of blue light onto its surrounding elements. If we were constructing this tweet button in the real, physical world, we might get a rounded rectangle of blue plastic and then put a single LED bulb under the plastic so it pulses in and out. This LED light would shine through the blue plastic and would cast a subtle blue glow to the table it's placed upon. Obviously we're not building real plastic buttons on tables, but this is exactly the look that is being emulated in this interface component.

I like to always think about my interfaces as if they were made of real materials, sitting right in front of me, with realistic attributes like highlights, shadows and glows. If I can't picture an interface element as a real, physical thing, then I probably messed up the design and my artificially-created lighting didn't do "the trick" of making me think it was 3D.

Next, we'll take a look at the Tweet button as there are a few interesting things going on. For one, this button is made to look bulged out so there's a single pixel highlight at the top, then it continues into a slight medium blue to dark blue gradient indicating a slightly convex surface. Surrounding the button is a subtle blue glow. If this were in the real world, that would indicate that this button is actually an artificial light source, glowing and illuminating objects around it. The subtle blue glow around the button is there to emulate a secondary light source, so it's giving off a blue glow onto the surrounding surfaces. The blue glow is very transparent, indicating that the light it's giving off is extremely faint.

Finally, take a close look at the "Tweet" text itself. Notice the subtle dark shadow at the top of each letter? This indicates that the text is sunken down into the button just a touch, but enough so that the top of each letter gets a top lip shadow due to the light being blocked there. When using text within an interface element, it's a good rule of thumb that it either sits on top of its surface or is sunken down into its surface, it's never on the same exact level. The point of this is that in real life, when physical objects have text on them, it has usually been struck and imprinted on the object, or painted and built up on top of the object. This extremely subtle depth difference (either down or up) should be recreated when you emulate real world objects and surfaces in your interface designs. This is typically accomplished with a single pixel highlight or shadow on the top or the bottom of your text.

Introduction To Layer Styles

Layer Styles in Photoshop are a way to add strokes, shadows, color & gradient fills, glows and more effects to your layers in a non-destructive manner. These Layer Styles can be applied, edited and saved very easily and are an indispensable tool for user interface design.

When you want to edit the Layer Styles for a particular layer, double-click on a layer (in the space to the right of its name) or select a layer and then click the fx button at the bottom of the Layers palette.

Recreating The New Tweet Window

The absolute best way to learn how to design realistic interfaces is to take an existing, good-looking UI widget and recreate it wholesale on your own. When you first start doing this, it may take awhile to get your recreation to be pixel perfect, but in the end all the work is worth it because you'll take that knowledge with you the next time you're designing an interface component. I learned how to design realistic, subtle interfaces by going into Photoshop with a screenshot of some great app (or even a Finder window!) and playing with the gradients, highlights and shadows until I could recreate it perfectly. Once you get the feel of recreating realistic lighting in your design tool of choice, it's like riding a bicycle, you never forget it.

Now that we've dissected the new tweet window's design, let's hop into Photoshop and build it.

To start, let's create a new Photoshop document about the same size as the aforementioned new tweet window. Next, grab the Rounded Rectangle Tool to create a vector shape that will be used to create the window.

Look At The Pixels

To be a really top-notch interface designer you have to look at pixels. It sounds obvious, but when I say "look at pixels" I mean zoom in as far as you can and look at each and every pixel to make sure they're correct and in the right place. To identify the very subtle design elements in Twitter for Mac's new tweet window I did exactly that: took a screenshot of the window and literally zoomed in until each pixel was a centimeter wide on my screen in Photoshop. To sweat the details in interfaces you have to pretend that each pixel is 10 feet tall, staring you directly in the face. Only then will you really and truly appreciate the pixel-level detail that the best UI designers in the world put into all their work.

The radius for the corners looks to be around 8px so that's what I chose after some trial-and-glancing-and-error. Why is it red? I made it red for the purposes of the screenshot, but that's going away very soon once we use Layer Styles to make it look better, so let's get into it.

In the Layer Styles window I dropped the Fill to 0% because the entire style will be managed by Layer Styles. Dropping the Opacity to 0% would hide the layer and all its Layer Styles completely, but dropping the Fill to 0% only hides the layer content, letting the Layer Styles continue to be shown.

First I added a Gradient Overlay between two shades of grey I retrieved from a screenshot of the actual window. The angle is 90° straight down because, again, the light source is directly up above according to Apple's interface guidelines. Next, I added a 1px Stroke along the outside of the shape. The color of the stroke actually isn't one solid color but a gradient from medium grey (#d5d5d5) to dark grey (#b0b0b0) just like in the real window. Finally I added a Drop Shadow to approximate the shadow that OS X adds to windows displayed on the screen.

With these styles applied (Gradient Overlay, Stroke and Drop Shadow) here's what our rounded rectangle now looks like.

It's starting to look like something! It's pretty amazing how quickly we can go from a red block to something that now looks convex, shaded and real, but that's the power of Layer Styles.

The real window that we're emulating isn't just a rounded rectangle with a gradient: it has the dark grey bar on the bottom that will hold the Cancel and Tweet buttons. To create the bottom area, I duplicated the main rounded rectangle and then used the Convert Point tool to turn the top two rounded corners into right angles. I did this by zooming in and clicking once on each point at the corner, then once the segment was no longer curved I used my arrow keys to slowly nudge the points into place as a perfect right angle.

Obviously the bottom area needs some attention so let's make it the right size (matched up with the actual tweet window screenshot) and then open its Layer Styles. I added a Gradient Overlay for the dark grey gradient, a 1px semi-transparent white Inner Shadow at the top (a slight edge highlight line), and then a 1px Drop Shadow straight up that's a simple, 1px bar shadow. It may not be obvious, but you can turn Inner Shadows, Drop Shadows, Inner Glows and Strokes into single-pixel style elements perfect for subtle edge highlights or shadows like we've done here.

And with that we're done with the main parts of the window. Not too complex, just two vector shapes with some specific Layer Styles. Here's what we have so far.

Now let's add the Twitter profile image to the top. After measuring the image, I found that it's 48x48 pixels with a slight rounded border. I pasted my profile image into the document, resized it, but it's not rounded yet.

There are a few ways to add rounded corners to a pre-existing layer, but I've found the easiest way is to draw a rounded rectangle using the Rounded Rectangle Tool and then remove the corners. I selected the tool, chose 3px as the radius, then drew a 48x48 pixel rounded rectangle directly on top of the image. To use this shape to cut out the corners of the image, I held down the Command key and clicked on the layer icon of the rectangle layer. This is a quick shortcut to instantly select all pixels from the layer you want without messing with the Marquee tool and precise selections. Here's a zoomed-in look at the marching ants selection.

Now that a nice rounded rectangle is selected, I switched back to the layer holding the profile image, inverted the selection (Select > Inverse) and hit Delete to chop off the corners and make it rounded.

In the real window we're emulating, the profile image is made to look slightly inset into the surface. This is accomplished with a subtle Inner Shadow at the top of the image (it's sunken in so light can't fully reach the top part of the picture) and a thin white Drop Shadow at the bottom (indicating the edge highlight after the sunken picture reaches back to the surface.)

After adding the tweet text to the window as well, the top part of the window is now complete.

At this point we should step back and take a look at our design compared with the original new tweet window. Here they are, side by side, with the original on the right.

Photoshop's text rendering isn't sub-pixel antialiased like on OS X but apart from that they look fairly similar with only minor differences in color apparent at high zoom levels. The only thing left now is to add the two buttons to the bottom area of the window, so let's get to it!

Let's begin with the blue Tweet button. I used the Rounded Rectangle Tool to create a vector shape for the button. Buttons are pretty simple: they're rounded rectangles. The key is what you do with the shape, and for that I'm using Layer Styles since you can easily adjust them later. The goal was to recreate the blue Tweet button so these are the styles I used to do that:

  • Gradient Overlay — Blue to lighter blue gradient with the same colors of the Tweet button
  • Stroke — Outer stroke, dark blue, 1px
  • Inner Glow — Very, very light inner glow with a 1px size and choke cranked all the way up so it's a solid, 1px line
  • Outer Glow — Semi-transparent blue glow because we're mimicking the Tweet button as being a second, artificial light source
  • Inner Shadow — The top edge of the button catches the global light source and needs to be a few shades lighter
  • Drop Shadow — The stroke at the bottom of the button needs to be a little darker to indicate there's a slight drop shadow.

I also added in the Tweet text to make the button complete. Just as I explained up above, there's a single pixel shadow at the top to indicate it's slightly sunken into the surface. To accomplish this I opened the Layer Styles and added a Drop Shadow just on the top of the text.

Here's what our homemade Tweet button looks like:

Not bad! It looks pretty good: very close to the original. It's fairly simple, actually, because it's just a slightly convex surface that exists within the context of a global light source shining straight down. Regardless of what kind of button or panel you design, it'll most likely have a top edge highlight, a slight gradient to indicate a bulge, and then a subtle drop shadow. This Tweet button is no exception.

I also dropped in the character count text to the left of the tweet button. It's white, but at only 28% opacity. To figure out the exact right opacity for various elements, there's no special process, it just involves adjusting the opacity slider ever-so-slightly until it looks the way it should.

The only element left in this window is the Cancel button. This has a fairly simple set of Layer Styles: a 1px semi-transparent black stroke, 1px semi-transparent white Inner Shadow, a faint Inner Glow, a 1px semi-transparent white drop shadow and a Gradient Overlay from white to transparent. A color-to-transparent Gradient Overlay is a common technique to use when your interface element is sitting on a non-white surface, letting the surface color show through the element. The opacity is cranked way, way down on the Gradient Overlay since we only want a faint highlight on the top of the button.

And for the big reveal, here's the final tweet window side-by-side with the original.

The new tweet window in Twitter for Mac is a great example of how a mixture of surfaces (both indented and pushed out) can interact to form a really cohesive whole. At first glance the window may look complex but by breaking down each individual element in Photoshop, it becomes a very manageable interface to design.

Calcbot

Calcbot is one of the best-looking calculator apps on the iPhone and iPad and is a great example of beautiful interface design that follows the Apple HIG.

There are a lot of elements on the screen at once, so let's break down some of the more interesting parts of this design.

First, and most noticeable to me, is the subtle application of noise across the main panels. In the real, 3D, physical world, the way that light interacts with a surface is dependent on the material and roughness of the surface. Shiny stainless steel is very smooth and reflects a lot of the light that hits it, whereas a rough paper surface absorbs more light. Adding noise to a surface in Photoshop is a common way to emulate a rougher, more textured surface than by just having a flat color.

Next, each button is designed to look convex (popping off the surface) but the thin, dark area around each button emulates an indented gap like when two metal surfaces meet. This slight gap makes each button pop more off the surface and look more like a physical, real object. Notice, too, that the gap has a very thin, very translucent white border around it as well. This is appearing to simulate a subtle edge highlight (the white border is whiter at the bottom, as it should be.)

Finally, the circular indicators on the bottom of the screen (to show which page the user is on) have a nice edge highlight at the bottom indicating that the ring is being hit by the global 90° light source, causing a slight illumination. This is fully in line with Apple's HIG and how this surface would look in real life.

Recreating Calcbot's Subtraction Key

The operation keys in Calcbot look similar to the numerical keys but to keep a visual separation they're colored blue.

To recreate this key in Photoshop, we should first plan our attack. Even though, as a whole, the shape is a single object, to accomplish the details we're going to need to use three rounded rectangle shapes: one for the black gap surrounding the key, another for the main blue key, and a third for the inner dark area.

I'm going to start by creating a new document in Photoshop and recreating the background noise. I filled a layer with a grey hue that I pulled from the original screenshot, then added some texture using Filter > Noise > Add Noise.

Next, after a little trial-and-error, I determined that the radius of the black gap rectangle (the outermost shape we'll use to recreate the subtraction button) is 12 pixels on the original screenshot so I grabbed the Rounded Rectangle tool and created a shape then filled it black with a semi-transparent 2px white outer stroke.

Here it is so far next to the original screenshot.

Now let's start the middle shape which is the meat of the button.

After identifying that there are 4 pixels in this "gap" surrounding the main button, I used the Rounded Rectangle tool with a radius of 8px (12 - 4 = 8) to draw the shape. When placing rounded rectangles within each other to create a built-up look, make sure to decrease the radius of the corners as you go inward to keep things consistent and realistic.

The Layer Styles used on this shape are very simple: a convex blue Gradient Overlay (with colors derived from the original screenshot), a 2px semi-transparent white Inner Glow, and then a slightly whiter Inner Shadow at the top to indicate a subtle edge highlight.

This is what our button looks like so far with 2 out of the 3 main shapes complete.

For the final rounded shape I decreased the radius once again down to 4px on the Rounded Rectangle tool. This shape has the simplest Layer Style of the three: a very low opacity black Color Overlay (to darken the blue gradient at the layer below) and a 17% transparent black Inner Glow with the Choke cranked to 100% (to make it solid, not fuzzy). And that's it!

The subtraction sign in the center of the button is a solid block with a shadow at the top, and after adding that, we're all done.

Screwing Up Lighting

It's incredibly easy to mess up a gradient or highlight and make an interface widget look totally unrealistic. I'd venture to say that most interfaces that have been designed with a realistic look as the end result end up not working at all. It takes a keen eye to create objects that mimic their real world counterparts.

To make sure that your gradients appear to be realistic, make sure to envision the type of object you're recreating before doing anything. Here are two slightly convex panels that are meant to look mostly flat with just a touch of bend, similar to the new tweet window we just created.

The panel on the left has a subtle, slight convex bend which means the color at the top of the panel is slightly lighter than the color on the bottom of the panel. There is a barely-there light edge highlight around the entire inside of the panel and the top has a brighter highlight. The panel also has a drop shadow that is correctly sized for this panel's simulated depth off the screen.

Rules, Schmules!

As anyone can tell you, some rules are meant to be broken. Apple's HIG states that a 90° light source will dictate how your surfaces and objects are lit, but not everything in your design has to appear fully realistic and rendered.

iPhone games will often eschew Apple's interface guidelines for their own unique look and feel. A common design trend for games is the bubbly, cartoonish look with thick strokes and few gradients, if any. This more illustrated feel runs counter to how interface elements in iOS are prescribed to look, but if a game is fully consistent with this look throughout the app, it can be used to great effect.

If you are going to run around Apple's guidelines, make sure that the visual world you create has its own rules regarding lighting and that you stick to them to keep your interface consistent from screen to screen.

The panel on the right leaves subtle in the dust and goes straight for unrealism. The gradient is far too sharp and indicates there is a giant ridge in the middle of the panel. The inner edge highlight is a sharp white, all the way around, which indicates an extremely bright light reflection that immediately stops once it reaches the panel. This just wouldn't happen if this panel were manufactured and placed on a wall in front of you. Finally, the drop shadow indicates that the panel is coming further off the screen than it should, as if there is some padding below the panel that is propping it up.

Real gradients, highlights and shadows are subtle. When something looks "off" in an interface, it probably looks fake, like it couldn't exist in the real world. When objects are lit realistically, they look like you could physically pick them off the screen. Always be extremely critical of your lighting effects to make sure they look and feel realistic.

Concave & Convex Styles

While designing interface elements, you'll typically want to use both concave (indented into the surface) and convex (bulging off the surface) visual styles. Alternating between these styles makes your website or software appear more balanced and more realistic since objects in the real, physical world are both indented and outdented.

A common interface design technique is to have an indented panel or area, and then the elements within this panel are outdented. Or, conversely, an area that appears to sit on top of the surrounding surface, but then elements within it are indented.

In Photoshop, we can easily recreate an indented panel by using the following Layer Styles:

  • A semi-transparent Inner Shadow located closer to the top of the panel to indicate the main light source is being stopped by the edge (remember the dimples on the golf ball?) and the area just under the edge is in a shadow.
  • A subtle Gradient Overlay where the top of the panel is slightly darker than the bottom of the panel. The bottom of the panel should appear to receive more light than the top since it's arcing back close to the surface.
  • A semi-transparent white Drop Shadow (just a single pixel) directly below the panel. This indicates that the surface is back to flush and the light source is hitting the lip, creating an edge highlight.
  • A thin, semi-transparent black Stroke on the inside of the shape. This sharpens up the edges and more clearly defines the Inner Shadow.

Here's a look at a rounded rectangle with exactly these Layer Styles applied.

The interesting thing about this particular look is that regardless of the shape, if it's meant to look pressed into the surface then it will look similar to this panel. Here's the same Layer Style applied to the panel, some text, and a cloud shape.

Now for the convex shape (bulging off the surface, towards your eye) the light bouncing off our object is doing equally predictable things: it's lighting the top part of the shape and casting a very subtle drop shadow. This is accomplished with the following Layer Styles.

  • A Gradient Overlay where the top color is lighter than the bottom color. This is because if the shape is bulging out, the top part of the bulge is catching the light and the bottom part of the bulge is sloping away from the light, so it's slightly darker.
  • A thin, semi-transparent white Inner Glow all around the edge will give a subtle indication that the light is catching the edge of the shape and causing an edge highlight.
  • A thin, semi-transparent white Inner Shadow at the top edge only shows a brighter edge highlight at the top where the light source is directly hitting our object. The transparency of the Inner Glow plus the Inner Shadow will make the top edge brighter than the other edges.
  • A subtle dark drop shadow cast directly down, but close to the object.
  • A thin, semi-transparent black Stroke on the inside of the shape. This sharpens up the edges and more clearly defines the Inner Glow.

Here are the same three shapes, but with this new, convex Layer Style applied.

Simple! Now that we have some nice convex and concave Layer Styles defined, let's combine objects that are both indented and pressed out and see how they look together.

They go together like peanut butter and jelly.

In the first example I combined an indented panel with concave text on top of it. This mixture looks makes it appear as though the text is actually rising up back to the surface and the panel is indented all the way around it. If this were manufactured in the real world, the indentation could have been made with a large metal press with the letters 'DTC' cut out of the middle.

In the second example the panel is concave and the cloud symbol looks pressed in, as if it's back at the surface. This embossed look is used extensively in Mac OS X for icon glyphs on top of convex buttons. Here's a screenshot of the Finder in Snow Leopard using exactly these techniques.

Learn Once, Apply Over And Over

The rules for realistic lighting in interfaces are not complex. There's a light source above your objects and the gradients, highlights and shadows you design should appear subtle and realistic, making your object look three-dimensional and real. There's an easy way to tell if what you're designing looks "real" or not: look at it! Does it look like it could be manufactured and exist in the real, physical world? Or does it look slightly off and slightly unrealistic? Can you name what type of material you're emulating or are you building something that couldn't exist in reality?

Designing within the Apple interface guidelines and adhering to a 90° light source may seem to be limiting at first, but it's actually very liberating and unlocks your creativity. Having no constraints at all may seem easier, but you're not designing interfaces in a bubble: your app has to be used next to every other app out there, and has to at least appear as though it fits in to the overall look of the platform. Being creative while still making your website or app look realistic and beautiful may be a challenge, but it's certainly a rewarding one. Good luck!
